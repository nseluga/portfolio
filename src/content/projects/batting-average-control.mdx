## The Problem

Batting average is simple — hits divided at-bats — and remains one of baseball's most-watched stats. Yet a .300 hitter one year might bat .260 the next without becoming meaningfully worse. BABIP luck, park effects, and small-sample noise all muddy the signal.

**The question this project answers:** How much of a hitter's batting average in a given season reflects repeatable skill versus circumstance?

This is a **skill-isolation problem, not a forecasting problem.** BAA is a same-season metric. It estimates what a player's batting average *should* have been given their peripherals — plate discipline, batted-ball composition, and sprint speed — not what it will be next year.

## Why It Matters

Batting average gets derided in modern analytics precisely because it conflates skill with luck. But the luck component is real and measurable. A .320 hitter with a .400 BABIP on weak contact is due to regress; a .265 hitter with elite contact quality and sprint speed is undervalued by the raw number.

BAA makes that distinction quantitative. The metric is indexed to 100 each season (± 10 covers roughly the middle of the distribution), so a BAA of 133 means the player's peripherals imply an average 33% above the league mean for that season — regardless of what their actual batting average was.

Face validity check: **Luis Arraez 2023 = 133.2** (elite contact rate, consistently puts the ball in play). **Joey Gallo 2023 = 61.2** (extreme strikeout rate, low contact frequency). The index separates them cleanly.

## My Approach

### Data

- **Source:** FanGraphs rate statistics plus Statcast sprint speed — 2015–2024 MLB seasons.
- **Scale:** 4,374 player-seasons across 1,173 unique players.
- **Filtering:** Minimum PA threshold applied per season; PA-weighted fitting gives more influence to qualified hitters.

Features used: strikeout rate, walk rate, contact rate, batted-ball type rates (ground ball, fly ball, line drive, pop-up), hard-hit rate, and sprint speed. Batted-ball rates are compositional (they sum to 1), so a **centered log-ratio (CLR) transform** is applied before modeling to handle the simplex constraint correctly.

### Model

A **mixed-effects model** (OLS base with player random intercepts via statsmodels MixedLM) fits AVG from the peripheral features. The player random intercepts capture stable between-player differences that the observed features don't fully account for — things like hand-eye coordination and bat path that show up in outcomes but not in rate stats alone.

The **intraclass correlation (ICC) is 24.7%**, meaning roughly a quarter of the variance in batting average across this dataset is attributable to stable between-player differences after controlling for peripherals. That's the signal BAA is designed to isolate.

Train-test split: random 80/20.

### Why Not Random Forests or Ensembles?

Both were tested. On BABIP prediction — a direct sub-problem — the linear model achieved a test R² of **0.372** versus **0.293** for random forest. The tree-based model consistently underperformed on this target. Ensembles were rejected on evidence, not assumption.

The linear model is also interpretable: **sprint speed is the #2 driver of BABIP** (after contact-quality features), which is analytically sensible and makes the model's internal logic defensible.

### BAA Index Construction

After fitting, each player-season receives a **`luck_component`**: the difference between their actual batting average and the model's expected batting average from peripherals. BAA is then constructed as:

```
BAA = 100 × (expected_BA / season_mean_expected_BA)
```

Indexed per season so cross-year comparisons reflect relative skill rather than era-adjusted run environments.

## Results

### Model Performance

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Test R² (AVG from peripherals) | 0.4570 |
| Test MAE | 0.0215 |
| Prediction interval coverage | 54.5% |

The prediction interval coverage of 54.5% is below the nominal 95% level — the intervals are poorly calibrated. This is an honest limitation: the model fits the mean well (MAE ≈ 21 points of batting average) but the uncertainty estimates are too narrow.

### Face Validity

The index produces intuitive rankings:

- **Luis Arraez 2023 — BAA 133.2:** One of the game's best pure contact hitters; his peripherals justify a high expected average, and he delivered.
- **Joey Gallo 2023 — BAA 61.2:** High-strikeout, low-contact profile; his peripherals predict a well-below-average batting average independent of BABIP luck.

Sprint speed emerging as the **#2 BABIP driver** (after contact-quality composites) is analytically meaningful: fast hitters beat out more infield hits and reach on errors, a real and repeatable skill the raw BABIP treats as noise.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Skill isolation, not forecasting.** BAA answers "how much of this season's batting average reflects repeatable ability?" It is not a prediction of next year's average. That distinction matters for how it should be used in player evaluation.

2. **Between-player signal is real.** An ICC of 24.7% confirms that players differ systematically in batting-average ability after controlling for observable peripherals. Ignoring that structure inflates error.

3. **Simpler wins on evidence.** Random forest and ensemble models were tested and rejected — the linear model outperformed on BABIP by 0.079 R² points. Model complexity should be earned, not assumed.

4. **Sprint speed is a legitimate batting-average driver.** Its position as the #2 BABIP predictor validates treating athleticism as a hitting input, not just a baserunning input.

5. **The natural extension is a stability test.** The model isolates same-season skill. The open question — how well does BAA in year N predict batting average in year N+1, net of the player's year-N peripherals? — would convert this from a descriptive metric into a forecasting tool and is the clearest next experiment.
