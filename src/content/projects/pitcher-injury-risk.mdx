## The Problem

Major League Baseball teams face a persistent operational challenge: predicting which pitchers are at elevated injury risk before it happens. Injuries are expensive — financially (medical costs, lost production, roster churn) and strategically (rotation depth collapses without warning). While pitch-count management is now standard, **quantifying individual risk requires integrating multiple data streams: workload accumulation, injury history, velocity trends, and rest patterns across a full season.**

Traditional rule-of-thumb thresholds (e.g., "don't exceed 100 pitches") miss the compounding effects. A pitcher carrying a prior IL stint and 900 pitches over the last 90 days is not the same risk profile as a fresher arm with identical single-outing counts. The question this project addresses: **Can a data-driven platform measure that difference systematically, and does that signal remain stable enough across seasons to be operationally useful?**

## Why It Matters

Pitcher injuries are:
- **Financially significant** — an ace on the IL costs $1–2M in replacement value per month.
- **Structurally predictable** — injury risk accumulates with workload, prior history, and velocity deterioration; it is not purely random.
- **Actionable if stable** — a signal that is consistent year-over-year can inform load management, trade decisions, and roster construction even when individual-game predictions are uncertain.

For sports analytics, this is a canonical hard problem: real data, binary outcome with class imbalance, strong temporal structure, and meaningful business stakes. Building honestly on it meant confronting the limits of the signal, not just optimizing a leaderboard metric.

## My Approach

### Data

- **Statcast (2015–2024)**: Every pitch thrown in MLB — velocity, movement, release extension, pitch mix.
- **Transaction and injury logs**: IL stints (10-day and 60-day) scraped and cross-referenced to build a binary injury outcome per pitcher-game, plus severity classification for multi-task modeling.
- **Derived workload features**: Pitches in the trailing 7, 28, and 90 days; rest-day gaps; appearances per rolling window; acute-to-chronic workload ratio (ACWR).
- **Velocity trend features**: Rolling mean, rolling standard deviation, and year-over-year delta — all computed per pitcher to capture chronic decline separately from game-to-game noise.

The final dataset covers **3,249 pitchers** across **205,911 pitcher-game rows** with **76 engineered features**.

### Methodology

The project is structured as a research platform rather than a single model. Each component addresses a different facet of the injury prediction problem:

1. **Baseline classifiers** — logistic regression and gradient boosting (XGBoost) trained on pitcher-game rows with stratified time-series splits. Established the discrimination ceiling under standard binary framing.

2. **Survival analysis** — Cox proportional hazards and a Random Survival Forest, modeling days until next IL stint rather than binary injury within a fixed window. Ensemble C-index: **0.566**. Survival framing handles the censored structure of mid-season data correctly: pitchers who finish a season without injury are censored observations, not negative examples.

3. **Multi-task severity model** — simultaneously predicts injury occurrence and, when an injury occurs, whether it will be a short-term (10-day) or extended (60-day) stint. Severity prediction lets the model distinguish between "this pitcher will probably miss two weeks" and "this pitcher is heading toward a season-ending event."

4. **Injury Risk+ (IR+)** — an ERA+-style composite scaled to a mean of 100 per season and pitcher archetype (starter vs. reliever). Computed across **9,752 pitcher-seasons**. Higher values indicate elevated risk relative to archetype peers in that season. Year-over-year correlation: **r = 0.583**, showing the score is stable enough to carry meaning across seasons.

5. **Probability calibration** — isotonic regression calibration applied to classifier outputs. Expected Calibration Error improved from **ECE 0.059 → 0.029**, meaning predicted probabilities are meaningfully closer to observed injury rates after calibration.

### Validation

All evaluation uses **temporal/walk-forward cross-validation** — training only on seasons prior to the test season, never leaking future data into features or the feature-engineering lookback windows. Walk-forward CV mean AUC: **0.571**.

That number is honest. Binary injury prediction at the individual pitcher-game level is genuinely hard: the outcome is rare, noisy, and influenced by factors unavailable in Statcast (soft-tissue imaging, off-field workload, proprioceptive deficits). A model that consistently beats chance on held-out future seasons, with well-calibrated probabilities, is a real signal — but it is not a solved problem.

### Feature Importance

Interventional SHAP analysis (conditioning on counterfactual workload paths) identifies **injury history and 90-day cumulative workload (`pitches_90d`) as the dominant predictors**. Notably, `pitches_90d` consistently outranks the acute-to-chronic workload ratio (ACWR) in partial-dependence magnitude, suggesting that raw cumulative load carries more signal than the ratio formulation popular in sports science literature.

### Key Findings

- **Chronic velocity decline is the strongest continuous risk indicator.** Pitchers with a documented year-over-year velocity decline exceeding 2 mph show an observed 30-day injury rate of roughly **12%**, compared to approximately **6%** for pitchers without such a decline — a near doubling. In IR+ terms, this cohort scores **119.5** vs. a baseline of **96.9** for non-declining pitchers.

- **90-day cumulative workload beats ACWR.** Interventional SHAP finds `pitches_90d` carries more predictive weight than the acute-to-chronic workload ratio. The practical implication: managing absolute accumulation over a medium-term window matters more than the ratio of recent-to-prior workload.

- **Injury history is the single strongest binary predictor.** Pitchers with at least one prior IL stint enter the model at elevated baseline risk that interacts multiplicatively with workload and velocity features.

- **IR+ is year-over-year stable (r = 0.583 across 9,752 pitcher-seasons).** This is the most operationally useful result: a composite risk score that correlates meaningfully with itself across seasons can inform roster construction decisions made months before the season starts.

- **Survival framing adds value over binary classification.** Ensemble C-index of 0.566 — modest, but consistently positive across walk-forward folds. Knowing expected time-to-injury, rather than just probability-of-injury-this-game, is more actionable for load management scheduling.

- **Calibration matters.** Raw classifier outputs underestimated injury probability for high-risk pitchers. Isotonic calibration (ECE 0.059 → 0.029) brings predicted probabilities close enough to observed rates that they can be used directly in risk-adjusted roster planning.

### What the Model Does Not Capture

- **Acute biomechanical breakdown** — Statcast measures pitch outcomes, not arm-path mechanics or muscle recruitment patterns. In-game form deterioration is invisible here.
- **Off-field factors** — training load, sleep, prior off-season surgery details, and proprioceptive testing are unavailable.
- **Individual resilience heterogeneity** — two pitchers with identical Statcast profiles may have fundamentally different injury susceptibility; the model estimates a population-level conditional probability, not an individual destiny.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Discrimination is genuinely hard at this task.** A walk-forward CV AUC of 0.571 is a real positive signal on an inherently noisy binary outcome, but it is not strong enough to replace expert judgment. The project's value is in the supporting outputs — calibrated probabilities, survival curves, and IR+ stability — not in a single headline accuracy number.

2. **IR+ may be more useful than raw predictions.** Because IR+ is scaled to archetype and season context and shows r = 0.583 year-over-year stability, it is more actionable for front-office decisions (trades, contract extensions, rotation planning) than per-game injury probabilities.

3. **Cumulative workload matters more than the ratio.** The finding that `pitches_90d` outranks ACWR in interventional SHAP challenges a common sports-science heuristic. It suggests tracking absolute medium-term accumulation should be a primary load management signal.

4. **Calibration is not optional.** A model with poor calibration is misleading in a risk-management context even if its ranking is decent. Bringing ECE from 0.059 to 0.029 via isotonic calibration makes the probability outputs trustworthy enough to use in downstream calculations.

5. **The multi-model platform is the contribution.** No single component — classifiers, survival models, severity prediction, IR+ — fully captures the problem. The value is in combining them: classification surfaces short-term flags, survival framing handles censored data correctly, severity prediction separates minor from major events, and IR+ converts everything into a stable, interpretable score analysts can act on.
