## The Problem

NBA analytics has popularized the "expected value" framework — quantifying shot quality by expected points rather than raw make-or-miss. But how well can a machine learning model actually predict whether a given NBA shot goes in?

This project, built with co-author Michael O'Brien, uses the 2014–15 season NBA shot logs (128,069 raw shots; 122,203 after cleaning) to answer that question systematically. We compared four classification models — logistic regression, decision tree, random forest, and XGBoost — across raw and engineered feature sets, then translated the best model's calibrated probabilities into an expected-value analysis by shot type and distance.

## Why It Matters

Shot-outcome prediction matters because expected value is a more honest measure of shot quality than raw field-goal percentage. A 40% two-pointer and a 33% three-pointer are not equivalent — expected points makes the tradeoff explicit.

More importantly, quantifying the **predictability ceiling** is as informative as the model itself. When four different algorithms all converge near the same AUC, that's signal: we've found the limit of what shot-context data alone can tell us, and that limit points directly to what's missing.

- **Contest pressure effects**: Does a tighter defender contest measurably suppress makes — and by how much?
- **Distance-adjusted EV**: Where on the court is the expected-value trough, and where does the corner-three geometry advantage actually show up?
- **Data limits**: Understanding what's missing from a public dataset is as valuable as what's in it.

## My Approach

### Data & Feature Engineering

**Source:** 2014–15 NBA season shot logs (Kaggle); 128,069 raw shots, 122,203 after dropping rows with missing defender distance or shot clock data.

The dataset includes shot clock, dribbles before shot, closest defender distance, touch time, and shot distance — but **no x/y court coordinates**. Feature engineering worked within those constraints:

1. **`defender_distance_ratio`**: defender distance divided by shot distance — normalizes contest pressure across shot types (a 2-foot contest means something different on a dunk attempt vs. a mid-range pull-up).
2. **Distance zones**: bucketed shot distance into restricted area (0–4 ft), mid-range (5–21 ft), and three-point range (22+ ft).
3. **Shot clock urgency**: binary flag for shots taken under 4 seconds remaining on the shot clock.

### Methodology

1. **Train/test split**: 80/20 random split stratified on outcome.
2. **Models compared** — both raw features and the full engineered set for each:
   - Logistic Regression
   - Decision Tree
   - Random Forest
   - XGBoost
3. **Evaluation**: AUC-ROC as the primary metric (binary classification: made vs. missed). Brier score for calibration quality.
4. **Calibration**: isotonic regression applied to XGBoost outputs to produce reliable probabilities for EV estimation.
5. **SHAP analysis**: identified which features drove XGBoost predictions most to validate the model's reasoning.

### Model Performance

XGBoost with engineered features achieved the best AUC of **0.6365** and a Brier score of **0.2296**, indicating well-calibrated probabilities. All four models converged near AUC 0.63–0.64 regardless of feature engineering, establishing a hard predictability ceiling.

The ceiling is informative: with shot-context features but no shooter identity, the model cannot capture individual shooting talent. A 35% three-point shooter and a 40% three-point shooter look nearly identical in the feature space. That gap — shooter skill — is the named missing variable.

### SHAP Feature Importance

**`defender_distance_ratio`** ranked as the top feature by SHAP value, confirming that contest pressure normalized by shot type is the strongest predictor in the dataset. Raw defender distance and shot distance were meaningful but weaker individually; their ratio captures the interaction more efficiently.

### Expected Value by Shot Context

Translating calibrated make probabilities into expected points (probability × point value) revealed:

1. **Open shots near the basket (0–4 ft) have the highest EV, around 1.85 expected points.** Restricted-area attempts dominate — low contest pressure and short distance reinforce each other.

2. **Mid-range shots form a trough.** Expected value for mid-range attempts (roughly 5–21 ft) dips below the corner three for equivalent contest conditions. The mid-range is genuinely inefficient relative to its alternatives.

3. **Corner threes (22–24 ft) show a bump in EV.** The corner three's geometry advantage — shorter distance to the basket than the wing arc — shows up clearly. Open corner threes sit in the same EV tier as restricted-area shots in terms of expected points per attempt.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The ceiling is a finding, not a failure.** Four models across two feature sets all converging near AUC 0.63–0.64 tells you something real: shot-context data has a structural limit. Shooter-level skill, absent from this public dataset, is the likely explanation.

2. **`defender_distance_ratio` dominates.** Contest pressure, normalized by shot type, is the single strongest predictor. The ratio encoding captures something raw distance alone misses: being tightly contested on a dunk is very different from being tightly contested on a step-back three.

3. **Calibration is what enables EV estimation.** A model with good AUC but poor calibration produces unreliable probability estimates. The Brier score of 0.2296 and isotonic recalibration step were essential for translating predictions into the expected-value conclusions above.

4. **Feature engineering improved reliability, not raw ceiling.** The performance gap between models narrowed after feature engineering, but the top AUC barely moved. Engineered features helped the model generalize more consistently across shot types rather than discover new predictive signal.
